## Motivation (mentor meeting, 2026-04-30)

Arpit asked a sharper version of the Phase 2 question: *what if the
agents had no personality bounds at all?* If hill-climbing had full
freedom of the [0, 1] probability space on every metric, would the
agents converge to a Nash-equilibrium "Oracle" profile, or would
they exploit their freedom to become more extreme?

This is a free experiment (no API spend) that tests the "bound boxes
are the binding constraint" claim from §6.3.

## Headline result: the trap *deepens*

Across 5 seeds × 10,000 hands with `ARCHETYPE_BOUNDS` replaced by
`(0.0, 1.0)` everywhere:

| | Bounded P2 | Unbounded P2 | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mean trust-profit r | **−0.637** | **−0.779** | **−0.142** |
| Std r | 0.125 | 0.087 | |

Per-seed:

| Seed | Bounded | Unbounded | Δ |
|---|---|---|---|
| 42   | −0.759 | −0.791 | −0.032 |
| 137  | −0.424 | −0.676 | −0.252 |
| 256  | −0.719 | −0.932 | −0.213 |
| 512  | −0.717 | −0.717 |  0.000 |
| 1024 | −0.564 | −0.779 | −0.215 |

Removing personality bounds makes the trap **worse**, not better, on
4 of 5 seeds. Mean r drops by 0.142, more than the size of the
P1 → P2 step itself (Δ = +0.115). The unbounded result is closer to
Phase 1 (frozen rules, r = −0.752) than it is to bounded Phase 2.

## Economic ordering: Firestorm runs away with everything

Per-archetype final stack across 5 seeds (starting stack = 200):

| Archetype | Stack (mean) | σ | Trust | Rebuys |
|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| firestorm | **7,861** | 892 | 0.405 |  0.2 |
| sentinel  | 1,422 | 284 | 0.807 |  0.4 |
| oracle    | 1,207 | 410 | 0.790 |  1.0 |
| judge     |   833 | 638 | 0.819 |  0.6 |
| predator  |   669 | 470 | 0.811 |  1.2 |
| mirror    |   336 | 182 | 0.794 |  1.2 |
| phantom   |   196 |  96 | 0.621 | 20.8 |
| wall      |   195 |  79 | 0.962 | 30.2 |

Firestorm ends with **40× the starting stack** — far more dominance
than in bounded Phase 2 (where Firestorm topped out at ~5×). Wall
and Phantom hemorrhage chips and rebuy 21–30 times each. The
"economic ordering" is now sharply tiered: one runaway exploiter,
five middle-of-pack agents, two bankrupt cooperators.

## Why the trap deepens

Three explanations, in increasing order of structural significance:

**(1) The hill-climber barely moves the parameters.** Inspection of
`phase2/adaptive/param_trajectories_unbounded.json` shows that
preflop bluff rates change by less than 0.05 across the entire
10,000-hand run for every archetype. With `delta = 0.03` and 25
total cycles per agent (each cycle picks one of 36 (round, metric)
slots), most slots are never touched. The hill-climber is doing
*local* search, and locally, every archetype's starting position is
already near a profit-maximum given fixed opponents.

**(2) When all eight agents simultaneously try to climb, the
gradient field is non-stationary.** A perturbation Sentinel makes to
exploit Phantom's prior round is evaluated against Phantom's
*next* round, in which Phantom may have shifted in response to
something Mirror did. The accept-reject decision is noisy and
positive perturbations get rejected as often as negative ones —
hence the ~50% accept rate across all seeds and the small parameter
drift.

**(3) The trust model is a stationary anchor that rewards
exploitation.** Every observer's posterior is computed from Phase 1
likelihood tables. Wall is still classified as Wall, Firestorm as
Firestorm, Sentinel as Sentinel. The trust-conditioned response of
opponents (folding to Firestorm's bets, calling Wall down with
weak hands) is therefore the same as in Phase 1 — even though the
agents now have full freedom to drift. The freedom is illusory: it
exists in parameter space but cannot escape the gravitational pull
of the static reputation ledger.

## Implication for §6.3 of the paper

The original §6.3 argued: "The bound boxes are the binding constraint;
within such a box, the climber cannot transmute Wall into Firestorm."
The unbounded result complicates this:

- The bounds **were** preventing the climber from making *destructive*
  changes (turning Wall into Firestorm would lose Wall its trust
  edge — but in this run, Wall doesn't even move because it can't
  find a better local profile against the existing trust model).
- The bounds were **not** preventing the climber from *cooperating*
  (it doesn't cooperate when freed; it just stays put because the
  current profile is locally optimal).
- The deeper binding constraint is the **stationary trust model**.
  As long as opponents' Bayesian posteriors are computed from frozen
  Phase 1 likelihoods, no amount of behavioral freedom changes the
  strategic equilibrium.

This sharpens the paper's argument: **the trust trap is not about
agents lacking the *capacity* to escape it. It is about the
*reputation system itself* being the trap.** When that reputation
system updates faster than agents can drift (Phase 3.1's per-opponent
memory + adaptive notes), the trap breaks. When agents can drift
faster than the reputation updates (Phase 2 unbounded), the
parameters are still constrained by the reputation system's
stationary expectations.

## Falsification of the "convergence to Oracle" hypothesis

Arpit's hypothesis was that unbounded agents would converge to
Oracle-like Nash play. The data falsifies this:

- Oracle's br stays at 0.32 (its starting value)
- Sentinel's br stays at 0.10
- Firestorm's br stays at 0.70
- Wall's br stays at 0.05

Nobody converges to anybody else. The hill-climber finds local
optima but not global ones, and the local optima preserve the
diversity that Phase 1 spec-locked in.

A stronger test would replace the hill-climber with a method that
can escape local optima — CMA-ES, REINFORCE with exploration noise,
or a population-based tournament. We do not run that here, but it
is a clean Phase 2.5 design.

## Caveat for the paper

The trust model still uses Phase 1's bounded likelihood tables.
Trust *posteriors* are therefore computed against the OLD
personality space even though *behavior* now lives in the full
[0, 1] space. The trust scores in this run are not directly
comparable to bounded Phase 2 — but the trust-profit *Pearson r*
is comparable because both endpoints (mean trust score, final
stack) are measured the same way. This is the same caveat as the
original Phase 2 redesign.

## What this adds to the four-tier ladder

```
Phase 1 (frozen rules)              r = -0.752
Phase 2 bounded  (bounded HC)       r = -0.637  Δ = +0.115
Phase 2 UNBOUNDED (free HC)         r = -0.779  Δ = -0.027 vs P1
Phase 3 (LLM personalities)         r = -0.510
Phase 3.1 (LLM + reasoning)         r = -0.094
```

The unbounded result sits *below* even Phase 1 — it is the
deepest trap in the entire study. This is a clean rebuttal to the
naive "give the agents more freedom and they'll cooperate"
intuition, and a useful piece of evidence that breaking the trap
requires *qualitative* reasoning capacity (Phase 3.1), not
*quantitative* parameter-space freedom (Phase 2 unbounded).
